1 / 11 · lead
We gave a folding robot more demonstrations and it got worse. Every extra demo still ended in a folded shirt. The data wasn't bad. It was noisy. The policy couldn't tell productive motion from dead time, and it imitated both. So which moments of a demo are actually worth copying? 🧵

2 / 11 · the problem
Every one of these demos succeeds. The mess is on the inside: dozens of hesitations, missed grasps, and recoveries tucked between the decisive moments. Delete the messy episodes and you throw away the re-grip after a near-miss, the recovery you most want the policy to learn.
media/training_diversity.jpg
3 / 11 · the flaw
To find those moments, you need a per-frame progress signal. The usual self-supervised recipe trains a model to predict how far through the demo each frame is (t/T). But elapsed time is not task progress. Three frames all stamped at the 50% mark show three completely different stages of the fold. Time tells you the clock ran. It doesn't tell you the task advanced. Progress reward models built on this signal inherit the noise.
media/noise_5050.jpg · all near 50%, three different stages
4 / 11 · WARP
WARP gets its labels from the demonstrations themselves. Take a short window of a successful demo and replay it at smoothly varying speeds, sometimes faster, sometimes slower, sometimes briefly in reverse. Because we set the playback, every frame comes with a progress label, with no human annotation. And because the windows and speeds are drawn at random, a single demonstration yields an essentially unlimited stream of self-labeled clips. The model learns relative progress, not absolute time.
media/ar1_draws.mp4
5 / 11 · the signal
On a held-out demo, the model predicts a dense, signed progress signal, answering a local question at every moment: forward, pause, or backward? When the robot drops the shirt, the signal goes sharply negative. A long stall sits near zero. Decisive folding reads strongly positive. The decisive moments are already in the data.
media/vhat_prediction.mp4
6 / 11 · WARP-BC
During behavior cloning, WARP-BC scores each action chunk by how much it advances the task. Productive motion gets amplified; stalls and regressions are filtered out before training. Same demos, but the policy now learns mostly from the moments that count.
media/warp_bc_method.mp4 · aggregation → reweight + filter
7 / 11 · payoff: speed
On the cleanest data tier, both methods fold all 20 shirts, so success is tied. But WARP-BC is much faster: 63.9s per fold vs 113.8s for vanilla BC, about 1.8x the throughput. Same demos, just spent on the moments that matter.
media/grid_tshirt_clean.mp4 · Vanilla BC | WARP-BC, cleanest tier, 2×
8 / 11 · payoff: robustness
As we admit messier, longer demos into training, vanilla BC's throughput craters. WARP-BC holds on the exact same data, training on just ~22% of the action chunks. Successful folds per hour, as the data gets noisier: • Vanilla BC: 32 → 1.5 → 0 • WARP-BC: 56 → 27 → 16 (~18x on the middle tier) (success rate falls the same way: vanilla 20/20 → 2/20 → 0/20, WARP-BC 20/20 → 19/20 → 14/20) It also outlasts prior curation methods on the noisiest tier (full comparison in the paper). The dataset was never bad. WARP-BC just learns which moments deserve to shape the policy.

9 / 11 · it generalizes
It isn't specific to shirts. Same method, new task: we pointed WARP-BC at bottle-in-bin, retraining the reward model once on bottle demos (task generalization, not zero-shot). Across both tasks, 420 real-world trials.
media/bottles_rollout.mp4
10 / 11 · bottles, head-to-head
On bottles, the same pattern holds: WARP-BC averages 11.3s per bottle vs 15.9s, about 1.6x the throughput, and places 74 of 80 vs 59 of 80. Keep the productive moments, and the policy gets both faster and more reliable.

11 / 11 · close
The instinct when a robot underperforms is to collect more data. The decisive moments are already in your demonstrations; the challenge is learning to recognize them.
